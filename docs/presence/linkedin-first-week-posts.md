## Post 2 — Technical teach: the DCF (Tue) · CTA: save + comment

**"Walk me through a DCF." If your answer runs past 90 seconds, you've lost them.**

Here's the whole thing, tight:

1. **Project unlevered free cash flow**, 5–10 years: EBIT × (1 − tax) = NOPAT,
   then **+ D&A, − capex, − increase in net working capital.**
2. **Discount each year to today at WACC.**
3. **Terminal value** — either Gordon Growth: final-year FCF × (1 + g) ÷ (WACC − g),
   or an exit multiple (EV/EBITDA × terminal EBITDA). **Discount it back too.**
4. **Sum** the present values: PV(cash flows) + PV(terminal value) = **Enterprise Value.**
5. **Bridge to equity:** − net debt (and preferred / minority interest) = Equity Value
   ÷ diluted shares = **implied share price.**

The math is rarely what dings you. *Reciting* it is. Say it out loud 20 times until
it sounds like you explaining, not you reading.

Save this for your next prep session. What part of the DCF trips you up most? 👇

## Post 3 — Demystify the process (Wed) · CTA: soft

**Most candidates aren't dinged for the wrong answer. They're dinged for how they got there.**

First-round technicals test less recall than you think. They test whether you can
**structure a thought, say it out loud, and hold up when the follow-up comes.**

Three quiet ways strong candidates still fail:

— **Silent thinking.** You solve it in your head; they hear nothing. Narrate.
— **No structure.** You start talking before you know where you're going. Frame first.
— **Folding on the follow-up.** The first answer was fine; "okay, and if rates rise?"
   wasn't. They're testing depth, not catching you out.

None of this is fixed by reading. It's fixed by reps.

## Post 4 — Behavioral teach: "Why banking?" (Thu) · CTA: comment

**"Why investment banking?" Nine out of ten answers are the same three sentences. Here's how not to be one of them.**

"I'm passionate about finance and markets" tells them nothing. A real answer has
three layers:

1. **The honest motivation** — you're drawn to *how companies make their biggest
   decisions*: M&A, raising capital, what something is actually worth.
2. **Why this seat** — the analyst chair specifically: real responsibility early, a
   brutal-but-fast learning curve, live deals and clients in year one.
3. **Your proof** — a deal you followed, a club you ran, a prior internship.
   Evidence, not adjectives.

Structure: motivation → why this seat → proof. Then say it out loud until it sounds
like *you*, not a cover letter.
